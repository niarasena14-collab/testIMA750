Electronic Disturbance

I found this weeks readings to be extremely captivating. Surprisingly so for me, as I tend to get a bit lost in wrapping my head around the technical theory of media and the inventions that make it what it is. Chapters 1 and 2 of The Electronic Disturbance lay out CAE’s core argument that contemporary power has become mobile, diffuse, and deterritorialized, no longer rooted in traditional institutions and I agree. In Chapter 1 the shift is described as the rise of a “nomadic” power structure; an elite that moves fluidly through global electronic and economic networks; making “conventional”, site-based political resistance increasingly ineffective and futile. Chapter 2 creates an addendum to the idea, The idea that the realm of virtual geography, arguing that because control now operates through digital and informational spaces, effective resistance must also move into these domains. CAE introduces the need for small, flexible, decentralized activist “cells” capable of performing electronic disturbance; symbolic, disruptive, or tactical interventions in networked systems designed to challenge or slow the flows of power that dominate contemporary life.

Race After Technology By Ruha Benjamin

Each week I find the course materials building on top of the other, bridging the gap between what we are doing in demos and what we can do and bring to our own projects! As a black woman, of course I must be fully transparent in saying I appreciate readings and moments in academia when the course material can relate to me or inform me personally on a deeper level, as it inspires a more indepth interaction with the work and with our art. This book is about how racism can be built into technologies—even when those technologies appear “neutral,” scientific, or objective. It reminds me of the conversations we have had with others pertaining to how people train search engines and AI to generate things. Inherently biased. Maliciously so? Maybe not, but still hard to say. Benjamin argues that digital tools, algorithms, and automated systems regularly reinforce existing inequalities because they are created within a society that already has racial biases. She calls this the “New Jim Code,” (I MEAN HOW FUCKING BRILLIANT) meaning a plethora of discriminatory designs that hide behind claims of efficiency, innovation, or data-driven fairness. Race After Technology is especially interesting to me as a Black female artist because it shows how the systems shaping our digital world aren’t neutral. Colorblind anything doesn't exist. That alone opens powerful territory for creative critique, resistance, and reimagination. Ruha Benjamin exposes how algorithms, design choices, and everyday technologies can encode racial bias, but she also highlights the radical potential of those who challenge these systems. As an artist, I truly draw inspiration from her analysis to explore how race, identity, and technology intersect in both visible and invisible ways. The book invites one to question who gets represented, who gets erased, and who gets misread in technological spaces, questions that can deepen my artistic practice and give me new frameworks for creating work that disrupts bias, reclaims agency, and imagines more liberatory futures.

Glitch Feminism and Josh's Theory of Radicalization

In the introduction, Russell explains that “glitch feminism” uses the digital realm as a space to challenge boundaries and escape the limitations placed on marginalized identities in the physical world. The glitch becomes a metaphor for breaking binaries: male/female, digital/physical, normal/abnormal, visible/invisible. She ties this to her own experiences and to broader histories of Black and queer resistance. The introduction sets the tone for the book’s larger argument: embracing the glitch; taking in instability, opacity, refusal, and fluidity which is (very much so) a feminist and anti-racist strategy for imagining new ways of being. Josh’s Theory of Radicalization by Joshua Citarella is an analysis of how young people, especially those active in niche/unique online communities, move toward increasingly extreme political identities. As opposed to focusing on traditional pathways to radicalization (like organized extremist groups), Citarella looks at how internet culture, meme ecosystems, anonymity, and algorithmic feeds shape political identities in ways that are fluid, experimental, and often ironic.

Joshua Citarella’s theory of radicalization and the introduction of Glitch Feminism by Legacy Russell come from very different worlds. One focuses on online political extremism, the other on digital feminist liberation. Although the blaring differences, they do happen to share some conceptual similarities that reveal how identity, technology, and digital spaces shape people today. Both focus on how identity is formed through digital environments, Both recognize that online spaces encourage fluid, shifting identities, Both emphasize the power of small, niche communities, and most importantly, Both critique the structures that shape digital identity.
